How to Unclog a Drain

What You Will Need

  • Plunger (cup-style for sinks, flange for toilets)
  • Drain snake or zip-it tool
  • Baking soda and white vinegar
  • Bucket and old towels
  • Rubber gloves

Step 1: Try Boiling Water First

Remove any standing water, then slowly pour a full kettle of boiling water directly into the drain. Wait 5 minutes. This dissolves soap scum and grease buildup ‚Äî the most common cause of slow drains. Do not use boiling water on PVC pipes if they are old or loose; use very hot (not boiling) tap water instead.

Step 2: Baking Soda and Vinegar

Pour ¬Ω cup of baking soda into the drain, followed by ¬Ω cup of white vinegar. Cover the drain with a wet cloth and wait 15-30 minutes. The fizzing reaction breaks down organic clogs. Flush with hot water afterward.

Step 3: Use a Plunger

Fill the sink with 2-3 inches of water to create a seal. Place the plunger over the drain and pump vigorously 15-20 times. The pressure dislodges clogs that chemical methods cannot reach. For double sinks, plug the other drain with a wet cloth.

Step 4: Snake the Drain

If plunging fails, insert a drain snake into the drain opening. Feed it in until you feel resistance, then rotate the handle clockwise while pushing forward. Pull back slowly to extract the clog. Zip-it tools (under $5) work great for hair clogs near the surface.

When to Call a Pro

Call a plumber if: multiple drains are clogged simultaneously (main line issue), water backs up into other fixtures, you smell sewage, or the clog returns within a week. Main line snaking costs $150-300 on average.
