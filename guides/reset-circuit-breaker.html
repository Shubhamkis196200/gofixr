Before You Start

A tripped breaker is a safety device doing its job ‚Äî it cuts power when a circuit is overloaded or shorted. Before resetting, understand why it tripped to prevent fires and damage.

Step 1: Identify the Tripped Breaker

Open your electrical panel. A tripped breaker sits in the middle position ‚Äî neither fully ON nor fully OFF. Some panels have a small indicator window that shows red or orange when tripped. The breaker label should tell you which area of the house it serves.

Step 2: Unplug Devices on That Circuit

Before resetting, unplug or turn off devices on the affected circuit. This prevents an immediate re-trip if an appliance is the cause. Common culprits: space heaters, hair dryers, window AC units, and microwaves sharing a circuit.

Step 3: Reset the Breaker

Push the breaker firmly to the full OFF position first, then flip it to ON. You should hear a solid click. If the breaker feels loose, won not stay on, or trips again immediately, do not keep resetting it ‚Äî this indicates a serious electrical problem.

When to Call an Electrician

  • Breaker trips repeatedly (more than twice)
  • You smell burning or see scorch marks on the panel
  • The breaker feels hot to the touch
  • Multiple breakers trip at the same time
  • Lights flicker in multiple rooms

These symptoms may indicate loose wiring, a failing breaker, or an overloaded panel. An electrician visit costs $150-300 for diagnosis but can prevent house fires.

Never touch the panel with wet hands or while standing in water. If you see exposed wiring, corrosion, or melted plastic inside the panel, close it and call an electrician immediately.
